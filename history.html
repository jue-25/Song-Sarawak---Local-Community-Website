HISTORY OF SONG, SARAWAK

Song is located by the Katibas River bank, a tributary of the Rajang River. It is a crucial stopover for river traffic going up the Rajang River. The district was named by the Kayan tribe as long, which means river stream. However, after the Iban tribe defeated the Kayan tribe, the district is owned by the Iban tribe and they renamed the place after an Iban warrior whose name was Song, he led the Iban tribe triumph against the Kayans. The Kayan tribes were the first tribe to occupy the Song District. At that time, the Kayan tribe were essentially nomadic people, meanwhile at the same time, the Iban tribes migrated from the present-day Kalimantan, Indonesia, and lived near the Katibas River Banks. Katibas River Banks is situated in the Song District. The Iban tribes lived here to find land for farming. However, the conflict between the Kayan people and the Iban tribes soon grew into a war, and the Iban tribes won. The Kayan tribes accepted their defeat and migrated to Belaga District.

In the 1800s Current Era (CE), the Chinese and the Malays arrived in Song District. They constructed wooden shophouses with nipah roofs along the river banks and started floating shops on the river. Initially, the barter system from Ancient India was brought in, but when the Kingdom of Sarawak took Song, a monetary system was brought in. In 1870 CE, the Brooke government built a fort at Nanga Song (mouth of Song river). The fort also functioned as the first administrative centre for Song and was intended to inhibit Iban uprisings at Katibas river. The Iban people opposed the introduction of the assessment tax by the Brooke government.

In 1937s CE, there were 10 Malay houses in Song. The Malay traders at Song were known as Abang (meaning “nobleman”).In the 1820s CE, they traded with the Ibans in Song in exchange for jungle produce. The Malays would sell the jungle produce in Sibau town which is known as Sibu in the present-day for a profit. Ships like “Ang Bee” from Singapore and “Kampar” from the Malay Peninsula wuld anchor in Sibu to carry the jungle produce back to their respective destinations for sale. In exchange, these ships brought daily necessities such as salt, sugar, salted fish, plates, bowls, pottery and clothes to trade with the natives. Meanwhile the Iban people worked as farmers, rubber tappers, and jungle produce collectors. The Ibans then sold their products at the Song bazaar in exchange for daily necessities. At that time, there were only 20 hut shops and all of the shops were built from planks and a nipah roof. Even though Song was isolated and a rural place, the business is very profitable.

However, the atmosphere began to become tense in 1944 when the Japanese army conquered Malaya up to the Song District. During the Japanese occupation in 1944, many Song people fled to Sengayan. During the journey to the destination, many of the refugees often experienced various obstacles and hardships and sometimes almost died after being denounced by the British government forces as refugees from the Song District.

The town of Song was destroyed by fire due to the attack of the Japanese army and cannon fire by the British Government. Only school buildings, mosques and Government Offices did not burn in the shooting incident. During the absence of government, theft was rampant after the Japanese military conquest. Most of the Iban people died after being shot by the Japanese. The Japanese army was greatly feared by the Chinese people. Therefore, they work together to protect the interests of their rights. During the Japanese occupation, most of the Chinese in Sibu and other small districts were afraid and fled to these districts. They were very afraid of the Japanese army who were known for their brutal punishment and torture. Most of them in this area felt the pain of living during the Japanese occupation. During the Japanese occupation, daily necessities were cut off due to a lack of export materials from foreign countries. The Japanese army is said to use force to get all the basic needs from the people.

At the time when Sarawak was trying to achieve independence in 1963, the Song Subdistrict Office had been an important operation room in the era of confrontation and dealing with the insurgency of Indonesian soldiers from North Kalimantan who infiltrated the border, and descended from Bukit Mubau to Sungai Ayat, Bangkit, Katibas. After the end of the Indonesia-Malaysia confrontation, Sarawak became independent on 16 September 1963 together with Sabah, Singapore and the Malay Peninsula, forming Malaysia, and in 1964 the first Sarawak Administrative Officer of the Song District Administration, SAO William Linang. Then, SAO Neilly Tanggai in 1969. Song became a district along with the declaration of Kapit as an administrative division within the state of Sarawak on 2 April 1973. The first Song District Officer was James Jimbun Pungga. On July 9, 1977, the Song District administration moved from the Song Old Fort to the Song State Administration building located on Song Hill, which is in the middle of Song Town.

Song town today has grown rapidly and still maintains its function as a stopover town. The development of Song District has caused the function of the office to increase, not only as an Administrative Center but also as a generator of Song District development by being a monitor and implementer of government projects at the district level.
